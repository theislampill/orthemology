## F. The Hume question — researched disposition

**Claim under adjudication** (from project-internal material): *Hume's account of the understanding is self-refuting — his denial that reason apprehends necessary connections is itself a piece of reasoning claiming normative authority, so the position undercuts the faculty it uses* (a "metaortheme self-refutation": using a governing standard to deny there are governing standards).

**Research basis.** Hume's primary texts: the sceptical argument about reason (T 1.4.1) — where Hume himself *anticipates* the tu-quoque ("if the sceptical reasonings be strong, it is a proof that reason may have some force; if weak, they can never invalidate the understanding") and answers it by reclassifying his sceptical arguments as causes rather than norm-governed refutations; the inductive argument (T 1.3.6; EHU 4–5); the mitigated-scepticism resolution (EHU 12). Scholarship: the naturalist reading since Kemp Smith (1905); Garrett (1997) on the Title Principle and the descriptive reading of the induction argument; Millican (2002) and Loeb on what the induction argument actually concludes; the SEP surveys (Morris & Brown; Henderson) reporting no scholarly consensus that Hume asserts a normatively self-applying global scepticism.

**Disposition: OVERSTATED (partially supported).**

- *Supported component:* against a **strong normative reading** — Hume as asserting, with the authority of reason, that reason has no authority — the self-refutation charge is classically forceful, and some of Hume's rhetoric invites that reading. As a dialectical point against *that Hume*, the project-internal argument stands.
- *Unsupported component:* as a verdict on **Hume's considered position** it fails, for three researched reasons: (1) Hume anticipates the charge and structures his response around it (T 1.4.1.8; EHU 12's mitigated scepticism), so the charge cannot be news to the position; (2) the dominant naturalist/descriptive readings take Hume's conclusions to concern the *causal origin* of belief (custom, not insight), not a normative prohibition issued by reason against itself; a descriptive thesis self-applies without self-refuting; (3) the scholarship offers well-developed consistent reconstructions (Title Principle; externalist readings), so "self-refuting" is not the settled scholarly verdict and may not be asserted as such.
- **Consequence for the companions:** the transcendental argument does **not** use "Hume is self-refuting" as a premise anywhere; Humean scepticism enters only as an instance of the priced A1/A2 retreat (a consistent position that abandons or reinterprets the normative starting point). The sentence "Hume is self-refuting" appears in no companion paper as a settled claim.
