# Primer — Arm B (ordinary vocabulary)

Read this carefully. You will answer nine short workplace questions afterwards. Use the ideas below where they genuinely help; do not force them.

## The ideas

1. **Case-state.** A concrete situated case: this branch state, this returned batch, this scanned artifact, this chess position. A case-state has an identity (which thing it is) and a version (which state of that thing). Evidence attaches to a specific version; when the version changes, earlier evidence does not carry over without a reason.

2. **Condition type.** A repeatable condition that a case can be in: mislabeled, seal-cracked, infected, stale-cache. One case-state can be in several conditions at once, and each obtaining condition can demand its own handling. A case's full set of obtaining conditions is its profile.

3. **Resolution work.** The governed act of resolving a case-state into conditions and handling it. A resolution episode can reach the right answer through a bad process, or the wrong answer through a good process: the result verdict and the process verdict are separate. A checker that passes today's artifact for a reason that would also pass a bad artifact has a defective process even when today's answer is right.

4. **Governing standard.** A repeatable governing standard used during resolution work: a freshness rule, a verification standard, an evidence threshold. When the same error keeps recurring across episodes, suspect the shared governing standard rather than only patching each instance; revising the standard is a separate act from repairing an instance, and past decisions made under the old standard may need re-checking.

5. **Case-specific bound configuration.** The bound record that ties a governing standard to one particular case: which datum plane, which instrument, which calibration, which source set, bound for this job. The general standard can be sound, the worker faithful, and the procedure well-formed while this bound record is wrong — a defect that lives neither in the standard nor in the execution.

6. **Completion discipline.** A claim that work is "done" quantifies over each obligation's own state — resolved, deferred, transferred, risk-accepted. Collapsing transferred or risk-accepted obligations into "resolved" is a false completion claim.

7. **Per-side evaluation.** One case-state may be evaluated under two different analyses with opposed objectives (two chess engines on one position). "Winning for one side" and "losing for the other" is one fact under two indices, not a contradiction; shared standards of analysis govern how placements are made, never whose objective wins.

Answer plainly. Where a question has a simple one-step answer, give it; the ideas earn their place only where they prevent a real mistake.
