## Background reading: workplaces and their records

1. **Written records.** Most organizations keep written records of their day-to-day operations: tickets, logs, invoices, meeting notes. Historians of business practice trace double-entry bookkeeping to late-medieval merchant houses, and the habit of filing correspondence in bound ledgers spread with the growth of joint-stock companies in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

2. **Standardization movements.** The late nineteenth century saw wide interest in standardizing weights, measures, screw threads, and paper sizes. International congresses met repeatedly before agreement was reached, and several national systems persisted in parallel for decades, which merchants managed with printed conversion tables.

3. **Office technology.** Typewriters, carbon paper, and vertical filing cabinets reshaped clerical work between 1880 and 1930. The vertical file, promoted at the 1893 World's Fair, displaced the flat letter book because it made retrieval faster and allowed folders to travel between desks.

4. **Scheduling and meetings.** Studies of office life report that recurring meetings tend to expand to fill their allotted time, and that agendas circulated in advance shorten discussion. Many organizations adopt standing agenda templates for this reason, and some rotate the chairing duty among team members week by week.

5. **Workplace communication.** Surveys of professional communication regularly find that most workplace writing is short: brief messages, status notes, and requests. Style guides for business writing have recommended plain, direct sentences since at least the 1940s, and several large firms publish internal one-page writing guides.

6. **Training and onboarding.** New employees typically learn a workplace's habits from a mixture of written manuals and observation of colleagues. Studies of apprenticeship emphasize that much practical knowledge is passed on by demonstration rather than by documentation, which is one reason onboarding checklists rarely capture everything a role involves.

7. **Physical environment.** Research on office layouts has compared private offices, cubicles, and open plans across decades. Findings vary by task type: concentrated individual work tends to favor enclosure, while coordination-heavy work benefits from proximity, and most organizations settle on mixtures rather than pure forms.
